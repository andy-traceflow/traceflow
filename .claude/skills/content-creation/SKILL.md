# Content Creation

Content is the slow burn. Direct outreach (see `marketing-copy` skill) drives clients 1-5. Content drives clients 6-20+. Both must happen in parallel from Phase 1 onward, even though content's payoff is 6-12 months out.

## The strategic role

LinkedIn content for TraceFlow has three goals, in priority order:

1. **Trust signal for outbound prospects.** When a contractor gets your DM and clicks your profile, your feed makes them trust you (or not). Content here doesn't go viral — it converts the 200 people who looked.
2. **Inbound leads slowly.** By Month 9-12, ~10-30% of new clients come from content. This compounds.
3. **Career optionality.** If you ever pivot back to job-applying or want partnerships, having "founder + 5K relevant followers" is a meaningful asset.

What it is NOT:
- A growth hack
- A short-term ROI play
- About viral hits

If you don't post for a week, you don't fall behind. If you don't post for 3 months, you've lost the compounding effect entirely. Consistency > brilliance.

## The cadence

**LinkedIn:**
- 3 posts/week minimum (Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday work best for B2B)
- 1 build-in-public post (what you shipped this week)
- 1 contractor-specific insight (something you learned about the niche)
- 1 mini case-study or before/after (real metric from real client, anonymized if needed)

**YouTube / long-form (Phase 2+):**
- 1 video/month minimum to start
- POV builds: "watch me build an AI lead recovery system for a flooring contractor in 90 minutes"
- Don't try to compete with established AAA YouTubers. Niche down to contractors specifically.

**Twitter/X (optional, low priority):**
- Cross-post LinkedIn highlights only
- Don't develop a separate Twitter strategy until Phase 3

**Blog (Phase 2+):**
- Long-form pieces from LinkedIn posts that perform well
- Optimized for SEO long-tail queries: "how to recover missed calls flooring contractor," etc.

## Post template: build-in-public

```
[Hook — first 1-2 lines must stop the scroll]

[Specific build or insight from this week]

[The mistake or non-obvious thing]

[What changed because of it]

[Optional: implication for the reader]

---

[Soft CTA — only sometimes; not every post]
```

Example:

```
Just spent 4 hours debugging why a contractor's lead chatbot was confidently telling customers we'd be there "Monday at 2pm."

Turns out the model was hallucinating availability from a placeholder business-hours config that defaulted to "Mon-Fri 9-5."

The fix: hard-validate every commitment-language phrase against the database before sending. AI confidence + missing config = customer expectations you can't meet.

If you're running any AI customer-facing tool, every promise it makes needs a deterministic source of truth behind it. Otherwise you're not running automation — you're running a refund factory.
```

That's a real, specific, contractor-relevant post that demonstrates expertise without being braggy or sales-y. Aim for this every time.

## Post template: contractor-specific insight

Pick a real pain point in the niche. Explain it clearly. Hint at how it gets solved.

```
[The pain — described from the contractor's POV]

[Why it persists — the underlying reason]

[The cost — in real dollars, not abstractions]

[The path — high level, not a sales pitch]
```

Example:

```
Surface contractors lose more revenue to missed weekend calls than any other source.

It's not because nobody works weekends. It's because answering services suck (generic scripts, no qualification, no urgency), voicemail loses 85% of callers (verified), and the contractor's spouse picking up at home is somehow still the most common Saturday morning lead intake system in 2026.

If you're doing $3M+ a year and not capturing your weekend inbound properly, the leak math is brutal: 10 calls/Sat × 30% would-have-booked × $4,500 ticket × 52 weeks = $700K/year.

There's a way to fix this with current tools — done it twice now. Won't pitch it here. But if you're nodding, we should talk.
```

## Post template: mini case study

```
Last month a [type of contractor] client [outcome metric].

Before: [specific painful state]
After: [specific better state, with numbers]

The thing that mattered most: [one specific insight, not five]

Happy to share the breakdown if anyone wants it.
```

Anonymize until you have explicit case study permission. Numbers matter more than names.

## What NOT to post

- Generic AI hype ("AI is changing everything!")
- Engagement-bait questions ("What's your favorite productivity tool? 👇")
- Vague platitudes ("Consistency beats intensity!")
- Other people's content reposted with your hot take
- Anything that says "Excited to announce..." without genuine news
- Threads of 12+ slides
- The em-dash-heavy AI-generated style that screams "ChatGPT wrote this"
- Anything that breaks the "show, don't tell" rule

## YouTube / long-form structure

When you start (Phase 2+):

**Video type 1 — "Build with me" (POV)**
- 30-60 min unedited or lightly edited
- One specific build session: "Adding a knowledge base to a tile contractor's chatbot"
- Show the actual code, the actual prompts, the actual debugging
- Audience: technical AAA founders, semi-technical contractors

**Video type 2 — "The breakdown" (analysis)**
- 10-15 min, edited
- "Why most AAA agencies fail at the contractor niche"
- "The 5 integrations every flooring contractor needs"
- Audience: aspiring AAA founders + contractor owners researching

**Video type 3 — "Case study deep dive"**
- 15-20 min, edited
- One real client's journey, with permission and anonymization
- Numbers, screens, conversations, outcomes
- Audience: prospects evaluating you

Start with one video/month. Quality > quantity. Use Descript or DaVinci Resolve for editing.

## Repurposing system

Every YouTube video produces:
- 3-5 LinkedIn posts (clips, quotes, key insights)
- 1 blog post (the transcript edited into prose)
- 5-10 short-form clips (for Twitter/X or LinkedIn video)
- 1 newsletter (eventually)

This means a single 1-hour recording session creates 2-3 weeks of content distribution. Necessary leverage.

## Voice notes for content

Same as marketing-copy skill: direct, confident, specific, founder-led. Plus for content:

- **More personal.** Marketing copy is about TraceFlow. Content is about Andy.
- **More didactic.** You're teaching, not pitching.
- **More vulnerable.** Real mistakes, real numbers, real "I didn't know this until..."
- **Less polish.** Slightly rough edges read as authentic. Don't AI-smooth everything.

## When to use AI for content

- **Drafting first versions:** yes, especially LinkedIn posts. Give Claude the hook + the bullet points + the voice samples. Edit aggressively.
- **Editing for tightness:** yes. "Cut this from 250 words to 150 without losing the insight."
- **Generating variants:** yes. "Give me 5 different hooks for this post."
- **The hook itself:** sometimes. AI is decent at 3-of-5 attempts. You pick.
- **Final pass:** always you, not AI. The last 10% of editing is taste.

## What success looks like at Month 12

- 50-100 LinkedIn posts published
- 8-12 YouTube videos shipped
- 2-5K relevant followers on LinkedIn
- 1-3 inbound leads per month attributable to content
- A library of repurposable assets

If you hit these, content is doing its job. If you don't post for 8 weeks straight at any point, treat it as a real strategic failure and fix the system (calendar reminders, batch-recording days, anything).
